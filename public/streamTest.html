Current Project: Underwater Diver Sled

I am currently working on this as a semester-long project in my Introduction to Robotics and Automation class. This underwater crawler is a vehicle for use by SCUBA divers and is outfitted with a special vortex drive that enables it to cling to a vertical surface under water. The goal is to refit the existing device with a controller that can be operated either on an observation deck above the surface or by a diver in the water. This project presents unique design challenges, since the device must be completely waterproof and the necessity of testing in the water limits the turnaround time between design tweaks and testing. More updates will come as the semester progresses.

Current Project: Peltier Cell Power Generation for the 3rd World

Peltier cells convert temperature differentials into electrical energy. The significant temperature differential between surface air and several inches below the ground can be exploited to hopefully produce enough energy to charge a cell phone or other small appliance. Growing reliance on cell phones in developing countries without equivalent expansion of the electrical grid leaves many people without a means to charge their phones, which may be linked to their livelihood. My group and I aim to build a low-cost, low-maintenance electrical generation device that could serve as a solution to this problem. This will be a year-long project, so updates are still to come.

Magnetic Levitation Control

The final project for my Control Systems class junior year was to program a controller to magnetically levitate a steel ball using the provided physical system. Using Matlab, Simulink, and Dspace, my group and I programmed a PID (proportional, integral, derivative) controller from scratch (the control diagram is shown below). Before we were able to start our controller, we also had to determine the mathematical model for the electro-mechanical system. The control system needed to be able to hold the ball steady at a range of heights, along with being able to move the ball in a sinusoidal pattern, a step pattern, and a random mode over the largest range of heights possible. After tuning, our controller performed all of these tasks quite well. Since we had extra time, we also tried a fuzzy logic controller, but that one was less successful, since we had less time to work on it. While completing this project, I had to learn how to use Simulink, as well as what it takes to acctually implement a control system.

Multi-Family Tornado Shelter

In my Engineering Sustainable Design class during my junior year, I worked on an interdisciplinary team to redesign the classic single-family tornado shelter. The goal was to design an economical underground shelter that could be used by multiple families in a low-income communities in tornado-prone areas. Having multiple families buy in to a single shelter would offset the cost, making it more likely for people to be able to be safe in the event of a dangerous tornado. As a starting point, we used a previous patent application from our project's sponsor, Bob Fischell. To make a successful product, we analyzed what was available from other companies, the laws and regulations in place, and the practical engineering and cost limitations. Another important design point was manufacturability in a factory, allowing for quick delivery and installation, leading a significant reduction in price. My main contributions to the project were using finite element analysis (FEA) to design steel doors that could withstand the required loads, 3D CAD design of the overall structure, weight calculations, and materials selection. Our final design accomplished all of our goals, presenting a product that could be manufactured offsite, be delivered, provide adequate protection for multiple families, and have a total cost of less than comparable shelters on the market.

DAQ-ATTAQ

DAQ-ATTAQ is a mobile data acquisition (DAQ) system that I designed and built for the mechanical engineering labs at Duke University. The DAQ systems are quite expensive, and are therefore traditionally bolted down to the lab tables to prevent damage and theft. But what about experiments in the field? My mobile solution is a protective. steel enclosure that never has to be opened by the end user and still allows full usage of the DAQ system. I took the project from the concept development stage, through design and materials selection (including catalog search & creating bills of materials), all the way to machining and assembly. After I selected components from the suppliers, I made up a 3D CAD model of each piece in SolidWorks and assembled it all digitally to plan the layout and ensure that everything would fit properly. The next steps were machining the enclosure, installing the hardware, and wiring everything together. This was my favorite project that I worked on while I was working with the Duke Mechanical Engineering Department, and it is still being used for experiments today.
